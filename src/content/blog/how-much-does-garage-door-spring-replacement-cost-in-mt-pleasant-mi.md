## First, a Quick Note on Spring Types

Not all garage doors use the same kind of spring, and the type you have affects the price.

**Torsion springs** are mounted horizontally above the door opening. They're the most common type on modern doors and are generally more durable and safer than extension springs. Most single-car garage doors use one torsion spring, while heavier or wider doors often use two.

**Extension springs** run along the sides of the door tracks and are more common on older or lighter doors. They're a bit simpler in design but tend to wear faster than torsion springs.

If your home has extension springs and you're replacing them anyway, it's worth asking about a **torsion conversion** — upgrading to a torsion system for better performance and longevity.

## Why Does the Price Vary?

A few things affect where your job falls in that range:

**Spring size.** Bigger, heavier doors need bigger springs. Larger springs cost more in materials, plain and simple.

**Single vs. double.** Two-car garage doors and some heavier single doors require two torsion springs. More springs means more parts and more labor.

**The condition of the rest of the system.** Sometimes a technician arrives and finds that the cables, drums, or other hardware are also worn and need attention. A good tech will tell you what they find and let you decide — they won't just tack things on without asking.

**The job itself.** Most spring replacements are straightforward. Occasionally a door has an unusual setup, limited clearance, or other factors that make the job more involved.

---

## Should You Replace One Spring or Both?

If you have two torsion springs and one breaks, it's almost always worth replacing both at the same time. Here's why: both springs were installed at the same time and have the same number of cycles on them. If one broke, the other isn't far behind. Replacing them together saves you a second service call — and a second labor charge — in the near future.
